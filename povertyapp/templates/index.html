Causes and Consequences of Income Inequality : A Global Perspective

Income Inequality


An imbalanced distribution of wealth within a country is associated with reduced educational and occupational choices for those who are poorest, as well as decreased economic growth for the country as a whole.

Understanding how income distributions differ globally is one step to addressing the issue.

What does income inequality look like globally?

How do we measure income inequality?


One of the ways we can talk about income inequality is to look at the difference in the share of income between those in the top 10% of earners, and those in the bottom 10% of earners.

In 2013, the largest difference in income share was in Brazil. Other South American countries were close behind.

The smallest differences were found in Europe; Ukraine, Iceland, and Norway had the smallest differences.

How has income inequality changed over time?




For countries with the highest income inequality, the difference between the income share of the top 10% and the bottom 10% has decreased over time.

The major exception to this is Zambia, which has shown an increasing difference in income equality.




For countries with the lowest income inequality, there are not consistent trends.

Ukraine, Czech Republic and the Slovak republic decreased their difference until 2007 and then stablized. However, the income inequality in Sweden and Denmark has increased over time.




The pattern of income inequality in the world's largest economies varies between the countries, but generally, the gap in most of these countries has stabilized from 2010.

The United Kingdom, Belgium, and Switzerland have seen the largest decreases over time.

Why is income inequality important?


As the income gap gets wider, differences between people of differing economic status become more structural, making it harder for lower income people to move out of their situation.

But income inequality should not just be a consideration for those who care about social justice. Work from the IMF shows that as income inequality becomes worse, a country's economic growth slows.
